# Give the Records Back

*Patient-Held Medical Data, the Pass/Fail Certification Layer, and the End of an Arrangement That Has Outlived Its Purpose*

**Bobby Bress**

*Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania · April 2026*

## Executive Summary

American patients are the only parties in healthcare whose access to their own comprehensive medical histories depends on the cooperation of institutions they did not choose. Their records are held in fragments across fifteen to forty institutional custodians who cannot or will not share them coherently, at an administrative cost to the system estimated in the tens of billions of dollars annually and an uncompensated time cost to patients running into hundreds of millions of hours. API-level access under the 21st Century Cures Act information-blocking rules has improved portal availability but has not resolved the underlying custodial arrangement, and the arrangement itself — built around the technological assumptions of 1996 — has failed on its own terms: the largest health data breaches in history have occurred at the institutions HIPAA trusted to hold the data.

This paper argues for a two-part replacement that uses technology costing forty dollars per patient and a regulatory model already working in aviation, commercial trucking, food service, and collective bargaining. Patients govern canonical custody of their own records on encrypted, biometrically secured devices, with synchronized mirrors held by providers, a designated backup custodian, and a standardized emergency summary layer. Third parties who need specific medical facts — employers screening for job fitness, insurers underwriting coverage, courts adjudicating claims — receive binary pass/fail certifications from qualified providers through a channel that makes raw data disclosure structurally unavailable through any authorized path. The coercion problem that has blocked patient custody proposals for two decades is defeated when the only channel through which employers and insurers can ask for health information produces certificates rather than records, with non-coercion statutes handling edge cases involving demands for device access or credentials.

The obstacles are political, not technical. Electronic health record vendors, insurance carriers, and the administrative-services industry built on fragmentation have interests in the current arrangement that run against the interests of both patients and practitioners. The reform aligns patient and practitioner interests against the specific incumbent interests that currently extract value from both, and the coalition that can move it forward is genuinely cross-ideological and cross-professional. Beyond the immediate costs the current arrangement imposes, record fragmentation is now the binding constraint on what medicine can learn about human health over the next century; the reform is a precondition for medical progress on a timescale that dwarfs any near-term transition cost. This paper specifies the architecture, documents the working precedents, makes the economic case to practitioners, dispatches the standard objections, and offers a four-statute legislative framework suitable for introduction.

## The Arithmetic

Before argument, the numbers. A complete medical history for a medically complex patient with decades of records, thousands of pages of clinical notes, hundreds of lab results, and dozens of imaging studies fits on hardware costing less than dinner:

- **Per-patient hardware cost, one time:** ≈ $40 for a biometric USB drive with 256 GB capacity
- **Typical record size, even for complex patients:** 5–10 GB
- **Triple-redundant custody (person, home, proxy):** ≈ $120–150, one time
- **Current cloud-custodian equivalent:** $120/year, perpetual
- **U.S. healthcare administrative overhead:** 25–31% of total healthcare spend, by multiples higher than peer nations
- **Annual administrative savings from genuine interoperability:** Tens of billions of dollars
- **Aggregate patient time lost to redundant records work:** Hundreds of millions of hours annually

The technical infrastructure to replace institutional custody with patient custody costs roughly $150 per patient as a one-time expense. The infrastructure it would replace absorbs orders of magnitude more in annual administrative waste. The security outcomes improve because centralized targets are eliminated. The only parties who lose are the incumbents who have built businesses on the fragmentation.

The transition itself has additional costs that the per-patient hardware figure does not capture: tech support infrastructure, device recovery services, the certified provider network for pass/fail determinations, standards development, and the onboarding labor at providers and at patient-facing distribution points. These costs are real, they are not hidden in this paper, and they are substantial in absolute terms. They are also dwarfed, by orders of magnitude, by the aggregate annual savings the transition produces once it is complete. The comparable case is the transition to Electronic Health Records under HITECH, which imposed substantial transition costs that were recovered in aggregate over the subsequent decade. The question is not whether transition costs exist; they do. The question is whether the long-run arithmetic favors the transition or the status quo, and the long-run arithmetic is not close.

The argument that follows is not that this transition is easy. It is that the scale of harm the current arrangement imposes has been misrepresented as the scale of harm changing it would cause. The two are not comparable.

## I. The Problem as Patients Experience It

Every American adult carries a data shadow distributed across fifteen to forty separate institutional custodians: primary care, specialists, urgent care visits from travel, dentists, optometrists, laboratories, imaging centers, pharmacies, insurance claims systems, employer wellness programs, and every entity that has ever generated a billable encounter. These fragments are individually incomplete, collectively inconsistent, and reconcilable only through labor the patient performs at every new point of care.

Twenty minutes of intake forms at each new provider. Specialist referrals that require either records transfer requests (which routinely fail, arrive incomplete, or arrive as faxed PDFs the receiving practice must re-key) or the patient reciting history from memory under clinical time pressure, with the gaps and errors that memory introduces. Every encounter starts over. Every chronic disease patient has had the experience of describing the same history to the fourth specialist in a treatment pathway because none of the prior three successfully transmitted it forward.

The cost to providers is equally real and less visible. A meaningful fraction of primary care encounter time is spent on records reconciliation: reviewing what the patient brought, chasing what they did not, entering data that already exists elsewhere in a format the current provider cannot read. Medical assistants and front-desk staff spend substantial portions of their working hours on records requests, insurance verification keyed to incomplete histories, and data re-entry that produces no clinical value. Clinician burnout, now a recognized public health problem in its own right, is driven in significant part by exactly this kind of institutional friction.

The security outcomes are poor by any honest measure. The largest health data breaches in recorded history have occurred at HIPAA-covered entities: hospital systems, insurance carriers, clearinghouses, and the electronic health record vendors themselves. Individual patients who lose a laptop expose their own data. Institutional breaches expose the data of millions at once. HIPAA's custodial model was designed to protect against the threat of covered entities misusing patient data; it has proven substantially more costly as a mechanism for concentrating attack surface than it has been valuable as a protection against the threat it was built for.

The response offered to patients who object is that they lack the expertise to maintain their own records. The claim is asserted rather than argued, and it does not survive examination. Americans maintain individual custody of birth certificates, Social Security cards, passports, property deeds, vehicle titles, professional licenses, tax records, mortgage documents, wills, and firearm permits — documents whose loss or compromise carries consequences at least as severe as medical records. The argument that this same population is specifically incapable of holding health data is inconsistent, and it persists because it is useful to the institutional parties who benefit from continued custody, not because it reflects any careful assessment of patient capability.

## II. The Structural Vulnerability

The coercion problem that motivates every serious patient-custody proposal has in recent years stopped being hypothetical. Institutional custody of medical data has become a mechanism through which state actors and private parties obtain patient information under legal theories and procedural shortcuts that would not reach the same data held by the patient directly. The category of Americans facing concrete risk from this mechanism is larger and more ideologically diverse than any single controversy suggests, and the structural vulnerability is the same across every category.

The vulnerability rests on a single doctrine. Under the third-party doctrine in American Fourth Amendment jurisprudence, data voluntarily conveyed to a third party receives substantially weaker constitutional protection than data the subject holds herself. Medical records held by covered entities are third-party records for this purpose, reachable under the HIPAA Privacy Rule's law enforcement exception at 45 CFR § 164.512(f) through administrative subpoenas, grand jury subpoenas, and written requests that fall well below the probable cause standard required for a warrant to the patient's own home or person. The Supreme Court's 2018 decision in Carpenter v. United States began to cabin the third-party doctrine as applied to sensitive digital records — specifically cell-site location data — and the reasoning of Carpenter applies at least as forcefully to longitudinal medical records, which reveal as much or more about a person's private life than location data does. That application has not yet been codified, however, and the case-by-case posture of post-Carpenter litigation leaves institutional medical archives as a significant ongoing Fourth Amendment vulnerability in the interim. This is the Carpenter gap, and it is the specific constitutional target the reform proposed here closes. This was an abstract jurisprudential issue in the era when the third parties involved were stable institutions with aligned incentives and a single legal environment. It has become an active vulnerability in the era when patients routinely receive care in multiple jurisdictions with divergent laws, and when institutional custodians face legal process from actors whose interests run directly against the patient's.

What this looks like in practice cuts across the political spectrum. Which example reads as most urgent to any given observer depends on that observer's politics; the structural problem is the same in each case.

### A Cross-Section of Who Gets Hurt

Reproductive health records have become the most visible current case. Patients who receive legally protected care in one state face investigation in another, with prosecutors seeking records held by covered entities through processes that would not reach those same records in the patient's direct custody. The state-by-state divergence in the legal treatment of reproductive care has made institutional custody a channel through which patients' choices are discoverable by actors who had no role in those choices and whose interests are adverse to the patient's.

Pain management records follow the same pattern. Chronic pain patients, disproportionately older and rural, face prosecutorial interest in their prescribing histories through investigations of their physicians. Records held by pharmacies, insurers, and treating providers are routinely subpoenaed in overprescribing cases, and the patient — whose treatment is legal, whose diagnosis is real, and who has no role in the alleged prescriber misconduct — finds her medical history in prosecutors' files without ever having consented to its production. This is a pattern that alarms populations and constituencies very different from those most concerned about reproductive care.

Mental health records carry comparable risk under multiple legal regimes. In states with aggressive red-flag laws, gun-owning patients have seen mental health treatment records used to support firearm restriction orders under procedures that would be constitutionally questionable if the records had been in the patient's direct custody rather than a provider's. In states with aggressive civil commitment practices, the same records support involuntary intervention. In child custody disputes, one parent's mental health history becomes leverage available to the other parent through discovery processes that access institutional records the patient would not have produced voluntarily. The populations affected span the political spectrum, and the mechanism is the same in each case.

Substance use treatment records, despite the enhanced protections of 42 CFR Part 2, continue to appear in child welfare proceedings, employment disputes, and immigration cases through channels that institutional custody enables and patient custody would not. HIV and sexually transmitted infection records appear in employment discrimination cases and immigration proceedings. Genetic testing results appear in life insurance and disability underwriting in ways that GINA's protections have not fully closed. Gender-affirming care records are the emerging frontier in states with aggressive investigative postures toward that care. Vaccination and non-vaccination records have appeared in custody disputes, employment actions, and school access proceedings in ways that have alarmed parents across the political spectrum depending on which direction the records cut against them.

> *The pattern is what matters, not any single case. Medical records held by institutions are discoverable under legal theories and procedural mechanisms that would not reach the same records held by the patient directly. The category of citizens currently at concrete risk from this asymmetry is tens of millions and growing. The specific controversy any given reader finds most salient is a function of the reader's politics; the structural problem does not depend on which controversy the reader cares about most.*

### The Principle Being Defended

What is at stake under these patterns is not the outcome of any particular policy controversy. It is a more foundational principle: that individual citizens in a representative democracy retain a zone of personal decision-making, particularly over their own bodies and medical care, within which the state's interest in their information is limited and procedurally constrained. The third-party doctrine, as currently applied to medical records, functions as a technical loophole through which the state reaches into that zone without the procedural constraints that would apply to reaching in directly. The custodial arrangement, not any particular legal theory, is what makes the reach possible.

Patient-held records, protected by the warrant-specificity requirements that apply to personal custody but not to third-party custody, close this loophole at the structural level rather than attempting to regulate it downstream through case-by-case protections that each depend on the specific legal theory being deployed against the patient. Records remain obtainable by law enforcement with a warrant specific to the patient, which is the constitutionally appropriate standard that has applied to personal documents since the Fourth Amendment was drafted. What becomes unavailable is the current practice of using institutional custody as a lower-threshold channel to obtain what a warrant to the patient would not produce as easily.

This is the argument that crosses the aisle when it is made on the principle rather than on any particular case. A reform agenda that leads with administrative efficiency does not build a coalition large enough to overcome incumbent resistance. A reform agenda that leads with personal privacy and individual self-determination in the face of state and private-actor reach into citizens' medical lives can. The coalition consists of patients who find different specific cases most salient, legal scholars concerned about the erosion of Fourth Amendment protection through technological and institutional change, civil liberties organizations across the spectrum, and citizens who are increasingly aware that the data trails their lives generate in institutional hands are available to actors they never authorized. That coalition exists. The reform agenda that speaks to it in terms of the structural principle rather than any single controversy can mobilize it.

## III. The Practitioner Case

The patient-facing argument for this reform is sufficient on its own to justify it. The practitioner-facing argument is that the current arrangement harms practitioners as well, and that reform aligns practitioner interests with patient interests in a way the current arrangement does not. The reform is not a gift to practitioners; it is a restoration of the pre-EHR relationship between a clinician's records and a patient's record, updated for the technology that now makes patient-held custody viable where it was not in the paper-chart era. Practitioners who understand what the reform does for their own practice economics have specific reasons to advocate for it that go beyond sympathy for patient concerns.

### A. What Practitioners Currently Pay For

Electronic health record licensing and maintenance is a major line item for practices of every size. A mid-sized independent practice pays substantial annual fees to Epic, Cerner, athenahealth, or similar vendors. A solo practice running a smaller system pays thousands of dollars per provider per year for the EHR itself, plus additional fees for interoperability modules, billing integration, patient portal functionality, and the compliance overhead that attaches to being a HIPAA-covered entity. Over the past fifteen years, these costs have been a major driver of practice consolidation: small practices have sold to hospital systems in significant part because they could not afford to maintain independent EHR infrastructure at the scale compliance now requires. The EHR vendor relationship has become the load-bearing economic relationship for many practices, and the terms of that relationship favor the vendor at every renewal.

Records request labor is pure overhead. Every practice of any size has staff dedicated to responding to records requests from patients, other providers, insurers, attorneys, and disability evaluators. This work produces no clinical value and no revenue; it is pure compliance cost attached to the practice's role as a covered entity custodian of patient data. The labor is significant, consistently underestimated in practice management planning, and absorbed directly into front-desk and medical-records staffing budgets that could otherwise support clinical work.

Prior authorization has become a crisis in its own right. Practices spend substantial staff time building authorization packages against patient histories the practice does not fully possess. Chasing prior records to support medical necessity arguments is now a routine function of clinical practice, and the work falls on medical assistants, nurses, and physicians themselves when staff resources run out. The American Medical Association has documented prior authorization as one of the top sources of practitioner dissatisfaction and administrative burden; much of that burden traces to the incomplete-history problem that patient-held records would solve at the point of service.

Documentation burden has produced a clinician burnout crisis that is itself now a recognized public health problem. Physicians currently spend roughly one to two hours on documentation and records work for every hour of direct patient care. This ratio is the proximate cause of burnout among physicians, nurses, and allied clinicians. It drives early retirement, attrition from primary care into higher-compensated specialties, and the pipeline crisis now visible across every major medical specialty. The documentation burden is partly a function of billing requirements that patient-held records would not change, but a meaningful fraction of it is reconciliation work against incomplete records that would be addressed directly by the proposed architecture.

### B. What Practitioners Would Gain

The custodial burden shifts. The practice still needs clinical documentation software, but the obligation to maintain, back up, secure, and make discoverable the comprehensive patient record moves to the patient. The software footprint required at the practice becomes smaller and focused on clinical work rather than custodial compliance. The licensing leverage EHR vendors currently hold over practices weakens because the vendor is no longer the indispensable custodian of the record; it is one of several tools the practice uses to support care. The economics of independent practice improve in a way they have not improved in twenty years, reversing the consolidation pressure that has squeezed small and primary care practices throughout the EHR era.

Records request labor largely disappears. The patient walks into the next provider's office with her complete verified history. The practice no longer needs dedicated staff time for the request-and-response infrastructure that currently consumes a substantial portion of administrative labor. Staff previously dedicated to records work can be redirected to clinical or revenue-generating functions, or the practice can run leaner. This is a direct operating-margin improvement that every practice manager will recognize.

Prior authorization becomes tractable. Authorization requests built against complete patient histories require less staff labor to prepare and have higher approval rates on first submission. The work that currently falls to medical assistants and nurses chasing prior records is reduced. Physicians spend less time personally engaged in authorization appeals that exist because the supporting history was incomplete in the initial submission.

Clinician time returns to clinical work. The documentation burden reduction is partial but meaningful: the reconciliation work against incomplete records is eliminated at the point of service, which returns hours per week to clinical practice, patient care, or personal time. This is the argument that moves individual practitioners at the level of their daily experience. The burnout literature is clear that administrative burden reduction produces measurable improvements in practitioner well-being, retention, and career satisfaction. Reform that addresses a meaningful fraction of that burden is worth advocating for on those grounds alone.

Malpractice exposure from incomplete history declines. A meaningful category of medical errors traces to missing history: the drug interaction with a medication the patient is taking but did not mention, the allergy documented in another provider's system but absent from the current chart, the prior imaging study that would have changed the interpretation of current findings. Complete patient-held histories reduce the frequency of these errors. Malpractice carriers price premiums against the underlying error environment; the effect on premiums would be delayed but real, and the underlying clinical safety improvement is immediate.

Value-based payment performance improves. Under the payment models that increasingly dominate Medicare and commercial reimbursement, practices are compensated in part on outcomes that depend on care continuity, readmission avoidance, and coordination across specialists. Patient-held records improve practice performance on exactly the measures these contracts reward. The revenue implication is positive for practices that have moved into value-based arrangements, and it weakens the financial argument for staying in fee-for-service for practices that have hesitated.

### C. The Practitioner Constituencies Who Should Engage

The practitioner coalition is not monolithic, and the argument does not land the same way for every subgroup. Honesty about this is necessary to build the coalition that can actually push the reform forward.

Independent primary care practices and small specialty groups have the strongest direct economic case. Their EHR costs represent a larger fraction of their revenue than for hospital-system-owned practices. Their records-administration burden is a larger fraction of their staff time. They are also the constituency most squeezed by current consolidation pressures, and reform that strengthens independent practice economics addresses an urgent concern in that community. The American Academy of Family Physicians, the American College of Physicians in its primary care advocacy, and the various state medical societies representing independent practitioners are the natural policy vehicles for this constituency.

Hospital-employed physicians have a more complicated position. Their individual practice economics are not their personal concern the way they are for independent practices; the hospital system pays the EHR bill. But the hospital-employed physician is the population most affected by EHR-driven burnout and most vocal about the documentation burden as a clinical quality issue. This constituency comes to the reform for quality-of-practice reasons rather than direct economic ones, and its advocacy operates through specialty societies and through the American Medical Association's broader policy positions on practitioner well-being and administrative simplification.

Nurses, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, medical assistants, and front-desk staff have interests that run in the same direction as physicians on the documentation burden but are less often consulted in policy debates. The American Nurses Association, the American Association of Nurse Practitioners, and the various allied health professional bodies are natural coalition members whose constituencies experience the documentation burden at close range and whose inclusion in the coalition broadens the policy reach substantially.

Hospital administrators and health system executives have genuinely mixed interests, and the paper should be honest about this rather than assume alignment. On one side, the administrative savings are real and large, and forward-looking administrators recognize the EHR vendor relationship as an increasingly lopsided one. On the other side, their institutions have significant sunk investment in current EHR infrastructure and significant vendor relationships that would be disrupted by the reform. This is a constituency to be engaged and won rather than assumed, and the engagement strategy should emphasize the long-run economics and the opportunity to restructure the vendor relationship on terms more favorable to the institution.

The specific incumbent interest to be overcome is the EHR vendor industry and the administrative-services ecosystem built around fragmented records. These are the actors whose revenue depends on the current arrangement and whose lobbying opposition will be organized and well-resourced. Practitioner coalitions advocating for the reform are advocating against specific corporate interests that extract value from both practitioners and patients, not against an abstract status quo. Naming the specific incumbent helps the coalition hold together under pressure, because the alignment of practitioner and patient interests against the vendor interest is real and defensible on the merits.

### D. The Realignment Argument

Under the current EHR-centric arrangement, vendor interests dominate, and practitioners and patients both pay the costs of serving vendor business models. The patient pays in records fragmentation, administrative friction at every encounter, and the structural privacy vulnerability described in Part II. The practitioner pays in licensing fees, documentation burden, records-administration labor, loss of clinical autonomy to the software's workflow, and the burnout that follows from spending clinical hours on custodial work. The reform aligns patient and practitioner interests by removing the vendor's position as the indispensable custodian and restoring the clinical relationship to the center of the arrangement.

This is the argument that wins practitioners, and it should win them because it is true. The interests currently served by the status quo are not practitioner interests or patient interests; they are vendor interests. A reform that shifts the custodial burden to the patient, reduces the practice's compliance footprint, and redirects clinical staff time back to clinical work serves practitioners and patients jointly against the common interest that has been extracting value from both. Practitioners who understand the arrangement in these terms have specific, defensible, economically grounded reasons to advocate for the reform, and the paper's coalition argument rests centrally on that understanding.

## IV. The Proposed Architecture

### A. Patient-Governed, Institutionally Mirrored Records

Under the proposed architecture, the canonical record of a patient's medical history is patient-governed: the patient controls access, authorizes disclosure, and holds the master key to her comprehensive record. The record itself, however, exists in multiple synchronized locations. The patient's personal device — encrypted at rest, unlockable through biometric authentication, and carried by the patient — holds the master copy that the patient controls. Every provider the patient has seen retains a cached working copy of the data that provider generated, subject to retention rules keyed to the canonical record. A designated backup custodian of the patient's choice holds a synchronized offsite copy. A standardized emergency summary card, readable without full authentication, remains accessible to emergency personnel under the break-glass protocols specified below. The patient's device is the canonical source the patient controls, but it is not the only copy, and device loss does not mean history loss. This is the distinction that matters and that the paper's architectural specification turns on: canonical custody is about who governs access, not about who holds the only copy.

This distributed pattern is how version control systems such as git work at scale, and it functions reliably for systems substantially more complex than medical records. Provider-to-provider queries under patient authorization retrieve working copies from any mirror in the network. A lost or destroyed device is replaced by restoration from the backup custodian or from the aggregate of provider caches, with the patient's cryptographic identity re-established through the credentialing infrastructure described below. The institutional high-availability properties that Tier 4 data centers currently provide are preserved in this architecture — they are simply preserved across a distributed network of provider caches and custodian backups rather than concentrated at a single institutional custodian. Geo-redundancy is achieved through distribution rather than through centralization. The emergency room case — an unconscious patient with no device — is handled by the emergency summary card, by queries to provider caches under break-glass authorization, and by the designated custodian layer. The architecture is more resilient under device loss than current institutional custody is under breach, because individual device loss affects one patient while institutional breach affects millions.

The cryptographic and identity-management technology required is mature, standards-based, and ubiquitous in analogous domains; specific standards selection among the available mature frameworks is appropriately handled by the standards body specified in the implementation pathway rather than pre-committed in this paper.

This architecture is deliberately distinct from the failed Personal Health Record attempts of the 2000s, and the distinction matters. Microsoft HealthVault (launched 2007, shut down 2019) and Google Health (launched 2008, shut down 2011) were centralized third-party silos that required patients to manually enter data or coordinate with providers who had no incentive to push data into them. They failed because they inverted the architecture precisely wrong — they made the patient a depositor into a centralized repository rather than the governor of a distributed one, and they required adoption effort from providers whose workflow and economics gave them no reason to participate. The architecture proposed here operates on the opposite design principles: data flows automatically from providers into signed cached mirrors as a byproduct of the clinical encounter rather than requiring manual deposit, the patient governs access rather than depositing data into someone else's silo, and provider participation is tied to the pass/fail certification infrastructure that gives providers direct economic and workflow reasons to maintain the cached mirror layer. The failures of HealthVault and Google Health are evidence that centralized patient-facing silos do not work; they are not evidence that distributed patient-governed architectures do not work, because no such architecture has previously been deployed at scale. The reform proposed here addresses the specific failure modes of those earlier attempts rather than repeating them.

### B. The Pass/Fail Certification Layer — the Load-Bearing Element

The central objection to patient-held records has always been that patient custody makes coercion easier: an employer who wants access to the data conditions employment on the patient producing it, and the patient either complies or does not get the job. This objection has been treated as fatal to the proposal for two decades. It is not. The solution has been operating in multiple regulated industries for longer than HIPAA has existed, and its generalization to the broader employment market is the key move that makes everything else possible.

Under the pass/fail certification model, third parties with legitimate interests in specific medical facts receive answers to specific questions rather than wholesale records transfers. An employer who needs to know whether an applicant can perform the physical demands of a warehouse position specifies a criterion — the ability to lift fifty pounds, freedom from active infectious disease, vision correctable to a specified standard, negative for controlled substances as defined by the position — and receives a binary certification from a qualified healthcare provider. The employer never sees the underlying medical data. The certified provider transmits only the fitness determination.

This is how Department of Transportation physicals work for commercial drivers, who receive a medical examiner's certificate rather than a transfer of exam findings. It is how Federal Aviation Administration medical certification works for pilots, whose airline employers rely on the certification rather than on examination data. It is how food handler health certification works across millions of workers in many jurisdictions. It is how union-negotiated medical screening has worked across decades of adversarial industrial relations, in which unions understood that employer possession of raw medical data created permanent vulnerabilities for members and built the intermediary-certification model as the durable solution. The infrastructure exists. The legal templates exist. The certified provider networks exist. What does not exist is the generalization to the broader employment market, and that generalization is the reform that unlocks patient-held records as a workable architecture.

> *The pass/fail model does not merely regulate what employers do with medical data. It restructures the transaction so the coercive demand becomes literally impossible to make. The employer cannot ask for raw data because no mechanism exists to deliver it; the certified channel produces only binary outputs. The friction shifts from "employee must resist an easy ask" to "employer must construct a non-standard ask" — which is precisely where friction should sit in an asymmetric power relationship.*

The principle generalizes beyond employment. Insurance underwriting becomes a qualified-or-not-qualified determination based on specified criteria, with raw data remaining with the patient. Life insurance medical examinations produce certificates of insurability rather than transfers of exam findings. Disability determinations produce eligibility findings rather than wholesale records disclosures. Court-ordered medical evaluations produce answers to the specific legal questions at issue rather than general records productions. The architectural principle is that third parties with legitimate interests in specific medical facts get answers to specific questions, and the patient's complete record never leaves the patient's custody.

### C. Coercion Resistance as a Design Requirement

Physical custody of a device creates one risk that institutional custody does not: the device can be physically coerced in a way a hospital server cannot. An abusive spouse, a predatory investigator, or a bad actor with physical access to the patient can in principle force biometric unlock in ways the hospital's firewall would resist. This is a real concern, it is raised most urgently by domestic violence advocacy organizations whose concerns the reform must take seriously, and the architecture addresses it directly as a design requirement rather than as an afterthought.

Three mechanisms operate in combination. First, duress codes: the patient registers a secondary biometric or PIN that, when used under coercion, unlocks a sanitized emergency-summary view rather than the comprehensive record, while silently flagging the access event. The comprehensive record remains unavailable to the coercing party, who sees what appears to be the full record without realizing the distinction. Second, time-delayed and co-custodian access for comprehensive retrieval: certain categories of sensitive data — psychiatric history, reproductive history, gender-affirming care history, substance use history — can be placed under a policy that requires either a time delay (during which the patient can cancel the release from any other device) or co-custodian authorization (a trusted third party who must approve comprehensive access). Third, and most important for coercion scenarios, the custodianship layer is available to any patient who requests it, not only to patients categorically unable to self-custody. A domestic violence survivor, a patient in a coercive family situation, or any patient concerned about physical coercion can place her canonical record under trusted third-party custody while retaining read access and authorization control from her own device. Coercion of the device then yields only what the patient has chosen to keep on the device, not the comprehensive record held by the custodian.

These mechanisms are not speculative. Duress codes are deployed in banking, security systems, and law enforcement applications. Time-delayed release is used in estate planning and in sensitive-document escrow. Co-custodian access is standard in corporate security, financial guardianship, and organ procurement. The coercion resistance the architecture provides is stronger than what institutional custody provides, because institutional custody is vulnerable to subpoena and social engineering in ways the distributed architecture is not. The domestic violence survivor whose medical history is held by a hospital has no recourse when her abuser's attorney subpoenas the hospital in a custody dispute. The same survivor under the proposed architecture has structural protections — custodian interposition, cryptographic consent requirements, warrant specificity — that are not available under the current arrangement.

### D. Emergency Access and Break-Glass Protocols

The unconscious patient arriving at a trauma center without a device is the test case every patient-custody proposal must pass, and this architecture passes it through the distributed mirroring specified in section A combined with a layered break-glass protocol specified here. The patient without a device is not a patient without a record. Every provider the patient has ever seen holds a cached copy; the designated backup custodian holds a synchronized offsite copy; the emergency summary card (often carried on a wallet card, a medical ID bracelet, or registered in a state emergency medical registry) is readable without full authentication. The receiving trauma team queries the provider-cache network under break-glass authorization, retrieves the synthesized current record, and proceeds with treatment. The architecture is more resilient in this scenario than current institutional custody is under regional disaster, ransomware attack, or the not-uncommon case of a patient transferred between health systems whose EHRs do not interoperate.

Pass/fail certification and biometric locks both assume a conscious, cooperative patient. Routine care meets this assumption almost always; emergency care does not. The architecture handles the non-cooperative-patient case through a three-layer break-glass protocol. First, a standardized emergency summary card readable without full authentication carries the clinically critical minimum: allergies, current medications, active diagnoses, advance directives, and proxy contacts. This information is available to any emergency provider without any device access at all. Second, designated healthcare proxies hold emergency access credentials that, combined with credentialed emergency personnel authentication and time-bounded access windows, unlock the comprehensive record for the duration of the emergency and log the access for subsequent patient review. Third, a trusted third-party escrow layer — analogous to organ procurement registries that already function in every state — holds encrypted emergency access keys releasable to verified emergency facilities under specified circumstances. None of this requires novel cryptography or novel institutional design. The analogous protocols already exist for financial account access upon death, safety deposit box access under power of attorney, and corporate security emergency overrides.

### E. Custodianship as Protection, Not Deferral

The custodianship layer is a core design commitment of the architecture, not a footnote to it. A meaningful minority of Americans cannot reliably maintain personal custody of important documents, for reasons that have nothing to do with their worth as patients or their right to medical care: cognitive impairment, severe mental illness, homelessness, active addiction, severe poverty with frequent displacement, the particular requirements of children and incapacitated elders, and the specific vulnerabilities of patients in coercive domestic situations. These populations disproportionately have complex medical needs, and they are currently disproportionately poorly served by institutional custody that treats them as interchangeable claims records rather than as patients. An architecture that fails them fails where medicine is most needed, and the reform proposed here takes the opposite commitment: the custodianship layer is designed to serve the most vulnerable patients at least as well as the current arrangement does, and in most cases substantially better.

The infrastructure already exists to build this. Federally Qualified Health Centers, community health centers, public health departments, and the community-based organizations that currently serve unhoused populations with mail forwarding, identity documents, and benefits advocacy are the natural custodian network. The Ryan White program infrastructure has provided analogous support for HIV-affected populations for decades. The Older Americans Act framework supports custodial services for elders with diminished capacity. These are mature, funded, professionally staffed institutional networks with established fiduciary obligations and existing relationships with the populations the custodianship layer must serve. Extending their mandate to include medical record custody for patients who need it is an incremental expansion of existing infrastructure, funded through the same public health mechanisms that currently fund these networks, not the creation of a new and underfunded parallel bureaucracy.

For children, custodial parents or guardians hold the record, with transition to patient custody at the age of majority. For adults under guardianship orders, the legal guardian holds the record subject to the same fiduciary obligations that attach to other aspects of guardianship. For populations with episodic capacity — early cognitive decline, mental illness in partial remission, recovery from active addiction — the custodian holds the record with explicit provisions for the patient to resume direct custody when capacity permits, and with the patient's own assertion of capacity as presumptively controlling absent a formal guardianship finding. For populations in unstable housing, community-based custodian services hold records as a public health good. For patients at risk of domestic coercion, voluntary custodial placement is available by request. In every case, the patient's agency over her own care is preserved to the maximum extent her circumstances permit, and the custodian's authority is scoped narrowly to the actions the patient genuinely cannot perform for herself.

The commitment the architecture makes to the most vulnerable patients is specific and verifiable: no patient under the proposed reform receives worse care, worse privacy protection, or worse institutional support than she would receive under the current arrangement. The custodianship layer exists to ensure that the reform's benefits reach every patient, not to create a two-tier system. This is a design requirement, a legislative commitment, and a test the reform must pass. A reform that improved care for the healthy and wealthy while leaving vulnerable populations worse off would not be the reform proposed here; it would be a failure of the reform. The framework described above is what the paper proposes to meet that test, and the legislation specified in Appendix B carries the commitment into statutory form.

### F. Identity Recovery Without Unfunded Mandates

A patient can lose both her device and her biometric access at the same time. A house fire destroys the device and the backup; a severe injury compromises the biometric recognition the device relies on; a period of cognitive impairment separates the patient from her credentials. The recovery path from this state is a genuine engineering requirement, and a poorly designed recovery path would push the burden onto community health centers and local clinics that are in no position to act as identity verifiers of last resort. The architecture addresses this directly rather than leaving it as an implicit unfunded mandate.

Identity recovery is tied to the federal identity infrastructure that already exists for comparable high-stakes interactions rather than to a new purpose-built verification system. A patient with a valid REAL ID, U.S. passport, or Social Security Administration verified identity can re-establish her cryptographic identity through the identity provider network that currently supports tax filing, Social Security benefits, veterans' benefits access, and other federal interactions where identity verification with equivalent stakes is already routinely performed. Login.gov and ID.me are the two existing federal identity providers operating at the scale this recovery path requires; either or both can serve as the trust anchor for cryptographic re-enrollment. The patient appears at an enrollment point — which can be a community health center, a post office, a public library, a DMV, or any other enrollment point certified under the Patient Health Record Custody Act — presents her federal identity document, completes the standard identity proofing protocol already in use for other federal services, and receives a re-issued cryptographic credential that re-links her to the backup custodian and the provider-cache network. The enrollment point does not verify identity; the federal identity provider does. The enrollment point is a kiosk, not a tech support desk.

This architecture preserves the burden allocation that already works in practice for other federal identity-dependent services. Community health centers are not asked to become identity verifiers for Social Security purposes; they are not asked to become identity verifiers for passport issuance; they should not be asked to become identity verifiers for medical record recovery either. The federal identity infrastructure that handles those other cases handles this one. The incremental cost to the existing identity-provider network is modest — it already operates at the scale required — and the incremental cost to enrollment points is the kiosk-level integration work that any new federal service occasions. For patients without qualifying federal identity documents, the same community-based identity document services that currently help unhoused populations obtain driver's licenses, birth certificate replacements, and benefits enrollment provide the bridge, funded through the same public health mechanisms described in the custodianship section above. The recovery path exists, it is specified, and it does not impose an unfunded mandate on clinical infrastructure.

## V. Answering the Objections

Patient custody of medical records has been proposed in various forms for twenty years and has not moved policy. The objections offered against it fall into categories of unequal seriousness, and the reform has failed in part because proponents have treated them as equally weighty. They are not. One objection has real force and must be dispatched directly; the others are secondary and can be answered more briskly. What follows weights them accordingly.

### A. The Coercion Objection

This is the objection that has actually blocked reform. Stated in its strongest form: placing records in patient custody makes employer and insurer coercion easier, because the custodial barriers that currently require third parties to obtain data through covered entities (with their institutional obligations to resist) disappear when the patient holds the data and can be pressured to surrender it directly. In the current legal environment, this is not a hypothetical concern; the willingness of state actors and powerful private parties to pursue health data through whatever channel is available, across the range of medical categories described in Part II, has been demonstrated repeatedly.

The pass/fail certification layer is the answer, and it is a complete answer, not a partial one. Under the proposed architecture, employers and insurers do not receive health data through any channel. The certified channel produces binary fitness or eligibility determinations. Raw data flows from the patient to the certifying provider and from the certifying provider back to the patient's record; it does not flow to the employer, the insurer, or any other third party. An employer who attempts to bypass this channel by demanding the underlying data from the employee is making a demand that has no delivery mechanism in the certified architecture and that a non-coercion statute makes per se improper. The friction flips from the employee's side to the employer's side, which is where friction belongs in this power asymmetry.

The DOT, FAA, food handler, and union precedents demonstrate that this works. Commercial drivers do not hand their medical records to trucking companies. Pilots do not hand theirs to airlines. Food service workers do not hand theirs to restaurants. The certifying provider sits between the patient and the third party, producing the document that addresses the third party's legitimate interest without exposing the patient's underlying health information. This has operated across industries for decades, has survived adversarial industrial relations, and has not produced the supposed inevitability of employer coercion that opponents of patient custody routinely invoke. The claim that it would fail under generalization to the broader employment market is an assertion, not an argument, and the burden of evidence falls on those making it, not on those proposing to extend a working model.

The objection that general employment screening has broader scope than DOT or FAA screening, and therefore creates more opportunities for employers to specify criteria that effectively require medical disclosure, is the one legitimate version of this concern. It is addressed by the regulatory body that specifies what criteria employers may and may not request — analogous to the bona fide occupational qualification doctrine under Title VII, analogous to the specific medical standards DOT publishes for commercial drivers. The doctrine exists. The mechanism exists. Extending it to cover the pass/fail certification criteria is a known regulatory task, not a greenfield problem.

### B. The Secondary Objections

***"Patients cannot be trusted to maintain their own records."***

Patients already maintain custody of birth certificates, Social Security cards, passports, property deeds, and vehicle titles. The claim that medical records specifically exceed this capacity requires justification that is rarely offered. The DOT medical certificate has been held in individual custody by hundreds of thousands of commercial drivers for decades without systemic failure. The custodianship layer addresses populations who cannot self-custody; the remainder of the population has demonstrated capacity with documents substantially harder to replace than medical records would be under the proposed architecture.

***"Providers cannot trust data handed to them by patients."***

They can, because the data is cryptographically signed at the source. Every lab result carries the signature of the producing laboratory, verifiable against a public key infrastructure. The trust is stronger than the current system provides, which depends on faxed paper trails and institutional reputation rather than cryptographic proof. The deployment is a coordination problem, solvable through federal standards, not an engineering problem.

***"HIPAA's privacy protections would be lost."***

They would be restructured, not lost. HIPAA's goal is patient control over medical information. Its current implementation achieves this indirectly, by restricting covered entities. The proposed architecture achieves it directly, by placing the patient in actual custody. The protections that need to survive — particularly the non-coercion and warrant-specificity provisions — need a live statutory home, and the amendments specified in Appendix B provide it.

***"The transition will harm patients who are not ready."***

A poorly designed transition would. A transition designed so the support infrastructure scales faster than the incapacity causes harm will not. The appliance-grade device with built-in redundancy and recovery protocols is the product category that must exist before full deployment; the thumb drive is the prototype. Five to seven years is the realistic window, with phased rollout and opt-in during the transition period. Patients who prefer institutional custody retain that option; the default shifts but the option does not disappear.

***"Incumbents will resist."***

They will, and this is not an objection to the proposal but a description of the political obstacle. Electronic health record vendors, insurance carriers, and the administrative-services industry have strong economic incentives to preserve the current arrangement and substantial lobbying capacity to do so. The reform requires a coalition large enough to overcome them: patients across the political spectrum harmed by the structural vulnerability described in Part II, small and independent practitioners whose viability is squeezed by EHR infrastructure costs, employers who would benefit from reduced screening administrative overhead, civil liberties organizations across the spectrum, and legislators willing to sponsor the four-statute framework. The coalition is genuinely non-partisan if the argument is made on principle rather than on any single controversy. Naming this coalition is the first step in building it.

A specific framing trap is worth naming in advance. Opponents will characterize the critique of vendor interests as hostility to innovation, as though the reform asked the health-tech sector to regress to paper records. It does not. The reform depends on exactly the technology the EHR industry developed over the past twenty years — the digital records, the interoperability standards, the cryptographic infrastructure, the clinical documentation tools. What the reform changes is not the technology but the economic arrangement under which the technology's benefits flow. Currently those benefits flow primarily to vendor subscription revenue; under the reform they flow to patients and practitioners, with the vendors retaining a legitimate and commercially viable role as clinical documentation and certification tool providers rather than as custodians of the patient's record. Opposition to rent extraction is not opposition to innovation. The health-tech sector that emerges from the reform is the sector that builds tools patients and practitioners actually want to use, rather than the sector that has captured captive customers through institutional lock-in.

## VI. Implementation Pathway

### A. Legislative Framework

The proposal requires federal legislation on four fronts, specified in detail in Appendix B. A Patient Health Record Custody Act establishes the legal status of patient-held records as canonical. A Health Data Certification Standards Act specifies the pass/fail framework and the regulatory body that administers it. A Non-Coercion in Health Data Access Act prohibits third parties from conditioning benefits on non-certified access and creates private rights of action with statutory damages. HIPAA Restructuring Amendments harmonize existing statutes with the new architecture, with particular attention to closing the third-party doctrine loophole that currently weakens Fourth Amendment protection for health data held by covered entities.

State-level legislation complements the federal framework on matters of state authority: professional licensing of certifying providers, state-level employment discrimination parallels, and the civil and criminal law frameworks for improper medical data access. Model legislation developed federally and adopted through uniform law processes offers the most efficient deployment path.

### B. Standards and Infrastructure

The cryptographic signing infrastructure is specified through a standards body with healthcare domain expertise — candidates include the Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology, NIST in coordination with HHS, and existing interoperability organizations such as HL7 and the Sequoia Project. The appliance-grade patient record device is a product category to be developed through private-sector competition against federal specifications requiring encryption at rest, biometric unlock, automatic multi-device redundancy, backup-to-custodian protocols, standards-compliant provider interfaces, and emergency access protocols that function without network connectivity. Device certification follows existing models from FDA medical device approval or Common Criteria security evaluation.

### C. Transition Timeline

Year one establishes the federal legal framework and initiates standards development. Years two and three complete standards specification, pilot deployments at volunteer health systems, and early device certification. Years four and five expand deployment through provider and patient onboarding, with the legal default shifting to patient custody as population coverage reaches threshold levels. Years six and seven complete the transition, retiring legacy covered-entity custodial obligations for patients who have moved to self-custody while retaining them for patients in the custodianship layer and those who affirmatively elect institutional custody.

This timeline is aggressive. A skeptical reader will call it optimistic. The response is that the technological components exist, the regulatory templates exist, and the comparable transition for Electronic Health Records under HITECH moved substantially faster than this once federal funding and mandates aligned. The limiting factor has been political will, not implementation capacity, and political will can compress timelines that technology cannot.

## VII. The Century-Scale Argument

Every argument made to this point has been about the present. The current arrangement imposes costs on patients and practitioners today that a better arrangement would not impose. The present-tense case is sufficient on its own, and a reader who accepts it has reason enough to support the reform. But there is a further argument that operates on a longer timescale, and this argument is the one that leaves opposition to the reform with no defensible public ground.

Record fragmentation is now the binding constraint on what medicine can learn about human health, illness, and disease. The tools for understanding etiology and mechanism — the genomic, proteomic, metabolomic, imaging, and computational methods that have matured over the past two decades — have outrun the data infrastructure on which they depend. Medicine has the instruments to understand disease at a resolution that was science fiction a generation ago, and the missing input is coherent longitudinal records: the actual course of actual patients, over decades, across providers, connected to the genetic and environmental context in which their health unfolded. The data needed to realize what the instruments make possible does not currently exist at scale, because the custodial arrangement under which it would be assembled fragments it at the point of generation and keeps it fragmented thereafter. Patient-held records are a precondition for coherent longitudinal data on any population large enough to yield the understanding the instruments can now extract. They are not a guarantee of that understanding — the research infrastructure, the consent frameworks, and the computational work must still be built — but they are the precondition without which the rest is impossible.

The objection that this framing sounds like surveillance enthusiasm is the objection a reader anticipates and an honest author must address. The answer is that the argument is the opposite of surveillance enthusiasm, and the position of the patient in it is what makes the difference. Surveillance means data collected about a person without that person's agency, used for purposes that person did not choose, by actors whose interests are not the person's. The architecture proposed here is the structural opposite: the patient holds the data, the patient chooses what contributes to research, the patient consents to the uses that aggregate her history with others. What is being argued for is not comprehensive data collection; it is coherent data stewardship under patient agency. The distinction is not decorative. The surveillance model produces the same kind of data through coercion and calls it progress; the architecture proposed here produces better data through voluntary participation and calls it what it is. Patients who have faced serious illness know this distinction from the inside. Those who have been through cancer treatment, long-term disability, or any other extended encounter with modern medicine have already had their privacy stripped away in every way that matters — the disease strips it, the treatment strips it, the forms strip it — and have learned that there are worse fates than biological disclosure to parties one has chosen. The patients whose histories would populate the research datasets that future patients would benefit from are not unwilling participants being surveilled. They are, overwhelmingly, willing participants who have asked — repeatedly, without being heard — for their experience to be used so that others do not have to go through what they went through. Surveillance is done to people. This is done by them.

The present author can speak to this directly. Twenty-five years of long-term care following cancer treatment and subsequent disability have produced the intake forms, the history recitations, the records chases, the incomplete-data encounters, and the administrative trauma that the first six parts of this paper describe. That experience is the reason the paper exists. But the same twenty-five years have also produced something else: the understanding that patients who have been through serious illness are, as a class, the most willing research participants modern medicine has. Asked to donate blood, tissue, family history, and data, they answer yes. Asked whether their suffering should contribute to the understanding that spares future patients, they answer yes without hesitation. Medicine's paternalism has treated these patients as objects to be managed rather than partners in the enterprise of understanding disease. The reform proposed here treats them as partners, and the partnership is what unlocks the century-scale argument. Patients who are respected as agents of their own care and as contributors to medical knowledge are the greatest untapped asset modern medicine has, and the reform that gives them custody of their own records is also the reform that makes their contribution to medical knowledge possible at the scale the instruments now demand.

The arithmetic on this horizon is not close. Over a hundred-year window, the number of future patients whose diseases are better understood, better treated, or prevented entirely because coherent longitudinal data existed where fragmented data now exists is enormous. The number of patients inconvenienced during the transition is finite and time-bounded. The utilitarian calculation, made honestly, favors the reform by orders of magnitude. This is the case for greatest happiness in the classical sense: aggregate human welfare served over generations by an infrastructure decision made now. It is also the case that forces the opposition into the open. A pundit who argues against the reform on administrative grounds can retreat. A pundit who argues against it on capability grounds can retreat. A pundit who argues that incumbent convenience should outweigh the medical welfare of future generations cannot retreat from that position without abandoning it, and the position itself cannot survive being stated plainly.

The AI era intensifies rather than complicates this argument. The medical specialties that will flourish in the next decades are those in which clinicians recognize patients as positive resources and partners — because those are the clinicians who will use the new instruments well, will earn the data contributions that make the instruments work, and will practice in a way that no machine can substitute for. The medical specialties that will be replaced by machines offering better-calibrated advice than a dismissive human provider are those whose practitioners have treated patients as problems to be managed rather than collaborators to be engaged. The reform proposed here is also, incidentally, the reform that distinguishes the clinicians who will thrive in the AI era from those who will be displaced by it. Patient-held records and patient-partner practice are the two halves of what good twenty-first-century medicine looks like, and the reform creates the conditions for both.

## VIII. Expected Outcomes and Conclusion

The administrative savings from genuine interoperability are estimated by multiple studies in the range of tens of billions of dollars annually in the United States alone. Patient time savings, valued at median hourly wages, represent tens of billions more in annual value currently absorbed by patients as unpaid administrative labor. Per-encounter costs decline because the records reconciliation step is largely eliminated. Provider-side breaches exposing millions of records at once cease to be possible because the centralized targets are eliminated. The attack surface shifts to individual devices, compromised individually rather than en masse, and the aggregate harm is lower.

Patient experience improves along every dimension that currently degrades it. Intake is shorter. Referrals proceed without records-chase delays. Emergency care benefits from a verified summary available immediately. Chronic disease management benefits from longitudinal records that follow the patient across providers. The general experience of being a patient becomes less administrative and more clinical. Provider experience also improves: clinicians spend more hours on clinical work and less on documentation reconciliation, which addresses the institutional friction that drives burnout in significant part.

Structural effects extend beyond the immediate clinical encounter. Electronic health record vendor lock-in weakens because the patient's record is portable by default. Patient ability to switch providers increases because the switching cost approaches zero. Independent and small practice viability improves as the EHR infrastructure cost burden declines. Insurance carrier leverage over patients through claims-derived information asymmetry declines. The market becomes more competitive at every level, which is a broadly desirable outcome that the current arrangement suppresses.

The case for proceeding is that the current arrangement wastes hundreds of millions of patient hours annually, absorbs tens of billions of dollars in unnecessary administrative overhead, produces the largest healthcare data breaches in history through its very structure, has become actively dangerous for tens of millions of Americans across the political spectrum whose medical histories — reproductive, mental health, pain management, substance use, genetic, and others — are reachable by state and private actors through the third-party doctrine loophole in ways that patient custody would close, and functions as the binding constraint on what medicine will be able to learn about human health over the next century. The case against proceeding reduces, when stress-tested, to a combination of incumbent self-interest and a paternalism about patient capability that is inconsistent with how the same patients are trusted with substantially more consequential documents in every other domain of civic life.

The choice between the current arrangement and the proposed architecture is not close on the merits. It is close only on the politics, and the politics are what the next phase of this work must address. The technology is available. The precedents are operating. The legislative templates are sketched. What is missing is the coalition willing to push past the incumbents who have shaped the discourse in their own favor for twenty years, and the willingness of legislators to take up a reform that delivers real benefits to their constituents against the preferences of the industries that currently profit from fragmentation.

Give the records back. The arithmetic, the architecture, the precedents, the political moment, and the century ahead all point the same direction.

## Appendix A. Working Pass/Fail Certification Regimes

The pass/fail certification model proposed for general employment and third-party access is not theoretical. It operates currently in multiple regulated industries, in some cases for decades, with demonstrated reliability. The precedents establish operational viability and provide templates for generalization.

***Department of Transportation Medical Certification***

Commercial driver's license holders undergo medical examinations by certified medical examiners listed on the National Registry of Certified Medical Examiners. The examining provider transmits a Medical Examiner's Certificate to the driver and, through federal registry systems, to relevant state licensing authorities. The driver's employer receives the certificate, not the examination findings. Underlying medical data remains with the examining provider and with the driver. This regime has operated for decades across hundreds of thousands of commercial drivers without systemic failure. The federal standards specifying what conditions are disqualifying and what accommodations are available are published and adjudicated through established administrative processes. The National Registry, overseen by the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration, is a lean, efficient credentialing model with defined rulemaking authority and established industry acceptance — not a sprawling federal bureaucracy. Generalization of the model to additional industries expands the scope of the certification criteria specified, not the scale of the administering body; the same pattern of narrow rulemaking adjudicated by a small expert office handles the expansion. The claim that pass/fail certification would require a vast new regulatory apparatus does not survive contact with the precedent's actual operating footprint.

***Federal Aviation Administration Airman Medical Certification***

Pilots undergo examination by Aviation Medical Examiners, who transmit certification findings to the FAA. Airlines and other aviation employers rely on the certification rather than on examination findings. The FAA maintains underlying records; employers do not receive them. This regime handles some of the most safety-critical employment screening in any industry and does so through pass/fail certification, not through employer-direct data access. If the model works for pilots responsible for hundreds of passengers at altitude, the claim that it cannot work for warehouse workers, office staff, or retail employees requires argument, not assertion.

***Food Handler Health Certification***

Many state and local jurisdictions require food service workers to hold valid health certifications indicating freedom from specified communicable diseases. The certification is held by the worker, verified by the employer, and supported by an examining provider who does not transmit underlying findings to the employer. The model operates at population scale across millions of food service workers.

***Union-Negotiated Medical Screening***

Collective bargaining agreements across multiple industries specify medical screening protocols under which union members are examined by designated providers, with results transmitted to employers only as fitness determinations for specified positions. This model emerged from union recognition that employer possession of raw medical data created permanent vulnerabilities for members. It has proven durable across decades of adversarial industrial relations and provides the direct template for generalizing the pass/fail model to non-union employment.

***Professional Licensing Fitness Determinations***

Licensed professionals in medicine, law, aviation, and other fields are subject to fitness-for-duty evaluations administered by state licensing boards or their designees. The evaluations produce fitness determinations transmitted to the licensing body; underlying medical findings remain with the examining provider. Employers and the public rely on the license status rather than on the underlying health information.

The consistent feature across these precedents is that third parties with legitimate interests in specific medical facts receive answers to specific questions rather than wholesale records transfers. The principle is well-established. The reform this paper proposes is the generalization of an established principle, not the invention of a new model.

## Appendix B. Legislative Packet

The following is structured for legislative staff. It identifies the four federal statutes required, their likely committee referrals, the existing statutes they build on or modify, and the fiscal argument each is likely to face. This is a structural specification suitable for preliminary drafting and constituent communication; full statutory language requires the ordinary legislative drafting process.

### Statute 1: Patient Health Record Custody Act

***Purpose***

Establishes the legal status of patient-held medical records as the canonical record for clinical and legal purposes, with institutional copies treated as working copies subject to retention and disclosure rules keyed to the canonical record.

***Likely Committee Referrals***

Senate HELP Committee (primary); House Energy and Commerce Committee (primary). Secondary referrals likely to Finance (for Medicare/Medicaid implications), Judiciary (for Fourth Amendment and privacy-law interactions), and House Oversight (which will claim jurisdiction over the federal identity-provider integration specified in the identity recovery section).

***Relationship to Existing Law***

Builds on the patient access provisions of HIPAA and the interoperability provisions of the 21st Century Cures Act, particularly the information-blocking rules finalized in 2020. Does not repeal these provisions; restructures their operation around the new custodial default. The ONC's Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) provides a deployable foundation, with the patient-governed node designated as a participant within the Qualified Health Information Network (QHIN) architecture. This frames the reform as the next step in the existing federal interoperability roadmap rather than a departure from it. The Federal Information Security Modernization Act (FISMA) governs the security posture of the federal identity anchors (Login.gov, ID.me, and the SSA verified-identity infrastructure) on which the identity recovery provisions in Part IV depend, and the statute coordinates with FISMA requirements rather than duplicating them.

***Fiscal Argument***

Scoring should credit administrative savings conservatively estimated at tens of billions annually across Medicare, Medicaid, and employer-sponsored coverage. Implementation costs are concentrated in standards development and device certification, both substantially below the savings ceiling. CBO scoring precedent from HITECH implementation provides a comparable framework for phased deployment costs. The statute anticipates that CBO will require a specific cost-per-enrollee projection for the custodianship-layer expansion described in Part IV; the statute envisions this projection being produced during legislative markup, in coordination with HHS and the community health center grant-making infrastructure, rather than pre-committed in the legislative framework itself.

### Statute 2: Health Data Certification Standards Act

***Purpose***

Specifies the pass/fail certification framework, enumerates categories of third parties eligible to request certifications, specifies which criteria may and may not be tested, establishes credentialing requirements for certifying providers, and creates the administrative body responsible for ongoing specification and adjudication.

***Likely Committee Referrals***

Senate HELP Committee (primary); House Education and Workforce Committee (primary, given employment implications); Senate Finance Committee (primary, given the statute's effect on medical necessity verification under Medicare and Medicaid reimbursement). Secondary referrals to Commerce (insurance implications) and Judiciary (administrative law provisions).

***Relationship to Existing Law***

Generalizes the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration medical certification framework (49 CFR Part 391) and the FAA airman medical certification framework (14 CFR Part 67) to broader employment. Does not disturb these existing industry-specific regimes; creates a parallel general-employment framework using the same operational principles.

***Fiscal Argument***

Minimal direct fiscal impact, designed for revenue neutrality. The certified provider network operates on existing healthcare reimbursement; the administrative body's operating cost is modest relative to the scale of the system it regulates and is funded through a user-fee model analogous to the FAA airman medical examination fee structure, under which certifying provider credentialing and ongoing oversight are supported by per-examination fees rather than general appropriations. A specific reimbursement code for the certification examination itself gives participating providers a direct revenue path rather than an unfunded documentation shift.

### Statute 3: Non-Coercion in Health Data Access Act

***Purpose***

Prohibits employers, insurers, and other specified parties from conditioning benefits on non-certified access to medical records. Establishes private rights of action with statutory damages. Specifies narrow exceptions for genuine emergency access and for court-ordered disclosure supported by warrant-specificity requirements. Includes safe harbor provisions for good-faith compliance, protecting employers and insurers from liability for the inadvertent receipt of raw medical data where the receiving party did not solicit the data and acts promptly to dispose of it; good faith is defined by reference to documented compliance procedures and is a question of fact for the private right of action.

***Likely Committee Referrals***

Senate HELP Committee and Judiciary Committee (joint); House Education and Workforce Committee and Judiciary Committee (joint).

***Relationship to Existing Law***

Amends Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, the Americans with Disabilities Act, the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act, and HIPAA. Closes the third-party doctrine loophole as applied to health data by treating patient-held records under the same Fourth Amendment standards that apply to other documents in personal custody. Includes surgical amendments to the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) to resolve preemption questions in the self-insured employer context, ensuring that the non-coercion provisions apply uniformly to self-insured and fully-insured employer health plans without disturbing the broader ERISA framework for employee benefit administration.

***Fiscal Argument***

Minimal direct fiscal impact. Enforcement is primarily through private rights of action rather than federal agency action. Savings to the employment and insurance systems from eliminated compliance costs associated with current data-handling requirements partially offset any enforcement costs.

### Statute 4: HIPAA Restructuring Amendments

***Purpose***

Restructures HIPAA's protections around the new custodial architecture. Preserves and strengthens the portions establishing patient rights (access, amendment, accounting of disclosures). Modifies the portions assuming covered-entity custody. Harmonizes GINA, ADA, and related statutes. Clarifies the treatment of patient-held records under the Fourth Amendment and state equivalents.

***Likely Committee Referrals***

Senate HELP Committee and Finance Committee (joint, given Medicare/Medicaid interactions); House Energy and Commerce Committee and Ways and Means Committee (joint).

***Relationship to Existing Law***

Direct amendment of HIPAA, HITECH, GINA, ADA, and related provisions. The amendments are extensive but the statutory goals are preserved; the implementation mechanics shift to accommodate the new custodial architecture.

***Fiscal Argument***

Net savings, as the restructured compliance framework is less costly than the current covered-entity compliance regime. CBO scoring would likely show substantial Medicare and Medicaid savings over the ten-year window.

### Constituent Communication Template

For constituents writing to their federal representatives, the core ask is: introduce or cosponsor federal legislation establishing patient-held medical records as the canonical custodial standard, supported by a pass/fail certification framework for employer and insurer access and by non-coercion provisions preventing bypass of the certified channel. The four-statute framework above specifies what the legislation must cover. Existing committee memberships of Pennsylvania's senators, and of the constituent's own representatives, determine which statute each is best positioned to champion.
